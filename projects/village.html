Marpha, Mustang district in Nepal
Himalayan valley, edge of the rainshadow region



I have been working with a local NGO, Marpha Foundation, on education and community projects since 2014



Located in a seat of one of the deepest gorges, on the banks of the Kali Gandaki river, Marpha is surrounded on one side by the Nilgiri range and by the Daulagiri range on the other. A strong community of the village that not only makes governing decisions but also enterprising ones for the welfare of the village are the Thakali. Their history with trade dates back to when salt from the Tibetan plains used to supply much of the Indian subcontinent. Being the wealthier families, it is their children who migrate to other parts of the country and world. The marginal community, that comprise most of the farm and house labour, are whose children will grow up to be the next generation of community. They attend the government school and grow up with a fear or hatred of learning. Our before and after school classes engage in unlearning the fear and building a sense of curiosity, creativity and play.


It has been an incredible journey building relationships with the children and the mothers over the years. Classes and projects begin from a conversation and continue as a creative collaboration with them. I am always conscious of gauging the relevance of themes and being sensitive of context. I am extremely grateful for all of my time there, including what experiencing the 2015 earthquakes taught me.



Some moments in Marpha.



My learnings include: ways of farming, the culture of villages in this valley, integrating into the community, creating workspace and recreational community space for volunteers, local and relevant methods of teaching different age groups, studying lunar and weather patterns, harvesting wild herbs, ways of fermentation, local recipes, waste management through art projects






Kannathampoondi, Tiruvannamalai district in Tamil Nadu, India
Hot, dry, rainshadow region of edge of the Eastern Ghats



Working with The Forest Way as part of their reforestation programme and at Marudam Farm School as a Theatre teacher and creative teacher for nature projects, 2015-2017



The village lies at the edge of a temple town and has been in the middle of constant flux over the decades since Independence to a more globalized time in south India. Still holding a stong linguistic and cultural identity of Tamilian life, the population is a mix of farmers, tradespeople, townspeople of various professions, foreigners and city-folk who have settled in this area. The Arunachala hill seems to draw people of some spiritual awakening, and there is a stirring of a movement back to organic practices of farming and other expressions of more simple ways of living. Tiruvannamalai also seems to have links to Auroville, another center of spiritual and cultural reawakening, just two hours away by road.



Some of my personal learnings have included:
integrating into a self-emerged community, learning about native species of trees and shrubs of the region, forms of plant intelligence, ways of forests, life in forests, creating exercises to engage senses in natural environments, gardening and permaculture, cooking with wood fire, baking, fermenting, forms of weaving, pottery and firing






Chennekothapalli, Anatapur District, Andhra Pradesh
Extremely hot, dry, arid region



Working with projects of the Timbaktu Collective, February-May 2015



The organization is well established in the region with many successful projects. The most impressive of which are reintroducing organic farming of millets in the region, running a school on the land and supporting local youth groups.



Some of what I engaged in and learned from:
conducting workshops for the local teachers, creating a system of waste segregation on the land, participating in workshops for local farmers, understanding harvesting and processing of grains for market, spending time at the weaving unit, participating in local festivals and gatherings/functions, fighting forest fires
